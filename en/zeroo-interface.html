( 1 ) Overview

Creating a system
from zero…o

This case study tells my experience of over 2 years in a startup's founding team. A true learning experience where I faced the challenge of being a one-person team, the mistakes and successes developed throughout the project, creation of rules and standards, application of UX theories to develop a cohesive system with four final products and, as a bonus, a visual identity.

( 2 ) Role

Unknown territory

After having my first experience in the public sector at an established company, I received a proposal to join the founding team of an idea. I took this challenge as an opportunity not only to improve my skills but to put everything I learned to the test in a fast-paced delivery environment with a small team.
Results
  • Experience in retail / Fintech: improvement in the marketplace sector, especially in price quoting, promotions, and economy.
  • Enhanced data visualization: creation of competent interfaces with a large amount of visual information crucial for the user.
  • Study in UX Research: improvement of my research skills in the area and user studies to refine screens, flows, and decisions.
  • Four products in one: improvement of the data management system (back office), creation of two apps for data sending and information visualization for the end customer system (front office), and finally, development of the main system.
  • Designer with a touch of developer: I learned a lot from my project companions. Through an agile perspective, we were together in developing new features for the system, usability testing, and using frameworks for time optimization. I also improved my skills in basic programming languages like HTML, CSS, and JS.
  • Maturation in project management: organizing all stages from initial wireframe prototyping to final prototypes. Defining demands and time management following the skeleton of agile methodologies.

( 3 ) Main challenges

The one-person team

Challenge
  • Navigating alone: developing, testing, analyzing, and improving a system from scratch as the only designer early in my career.
  • Four times more work: developing screens for four products with different functions and formats. These being: a database, two applications, and the main system.
  • Time is precious: lack of space for initial usability tests due to lack of time, low initial budget, and need to demonstrate professional value quickly.
Solution
  • Not reinventing the wheel: construction of interface alternatives based on metrics and free libraries already established like Material Design and Font Awesome.
  • At a fast pace: rapid prototyping of screens and features for usability testing, implementation, and system improvement.
  • The most important first: a complex system must have its basics solid. My focus was to make the most important flow of the site as cohesive and functional as possible. Thus, features orbiting this system would have a solid foundation on which they could be built and tested efficiently.
  • Invisible value: whenever possible, highlight the value of UX in visual elements. Giving more space for research and collection of quantitative and qualitative data.

( 4 ) Development

To battle!
Or almost that...

Deliver first,
detail later From the project's conception, there was a certainty: some things would become UX debt. With time running and several developers ready for the new system implementation, I had to choose what would be the priority and which screens would be refined later. I chose to focus on the system's main functionality, approximately 40% of the final product, since the remaining 60% depended on it at some point in their flows.
Predictably
necessary Much of the UX was based on Jakob's Law. There was no time or personnel to develop all elements, so it was necessary to adopt functionalities that had already been tested and refined. Elements and guidelines from Excel, Kayak, Ifood, and other market giants were essential for the conception of assets, grids, and system behaviors.
Breadcrumb facilitates user flow.
Button states and their behaviors
Test, research
and more test After finishing the first version, I turned my attention to the user. Fortunately, the general functionality of the system was understood, lacking some adjustments that were improved and then tested again. UX finally became the big star.
Complexity
facilitated Once user feedback was absorbed into the system, new functionalities and elements were improved based on research, real data, and pains external to empiricism. Thus, the system consolidated with a concrete and effective foundation. An MVP with the look of a final product.

( 4.1 ) Startup brand

Leaving the comfort
zone

Creating an identity for a brand is always a significant challenge, and in the case of the Zeroo brand, it was no different. Although my role in the company is as a Product Designer, I stepped completely out of my comfort zone to develop an identity that reflected the values and essence of this project, for which I have great appreciation. After several meetings with the Product Owners (POs), we reached a concept that not only represents the pillars of the company but also conveys a welcoming and innovative image of a product that aims to revolutionize the restaurant industry. It was a challenging but extremely rewarding process that resulted in a visual identity aligned with the transformation vision that Zeroo seeks to promote.

The development of the Zeroo brand icon was a careful and detailed process that required attention to both aesthetics and functionality. As the icon would be the visual representation of the brand in such a reduced and frequently used space, it was essential that it be simple, memorable, and aligned with the company's values. The main idea is a stylized "Z" in the shape of a shopping cart.

( 5 ) The end of a chapter

"A gigantic professional growth. Many hours of research, many coffees, and many lessons that I will certainly take in my professional baggage as a Product Designer"
Learnings
  • Finishing alone: developing four projects was a huge responsibility and being able to finish was something even greater.
  • Growth as a designer: problem-solving, activity management, and the power of communication were key elements to finishing this project.
  • A professional is always a learner: knowing that you don't know and being willing to improve is the key to maturation. It was vital to seek more information, books, articles, and reports from other designers in the same situation.
  • A good interface is the result of good UX: the user is the key to an efficient and real system. A beautiful interface has its function but must communicate well with those who will use it.
  • Rapid development: being able to develop and progress in an environment with constant changes and generation of quick alternatives.
