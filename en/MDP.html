( 1 ) Overview

A Pedagogical Directional Material

This case study reports the development of my final graduation project (TCC), an interactive PDF to assist in the conception of digital games for the Game Design course at the University of Brasília (UnB). The process of creating a product for a broad audience, the development of an interaction design with effective pedagogical function, and the creation of a final product that draws heavily on UX principles, but outside the logic of app conception.

( 2 ) Role

A real gameplay

As this project was my final graduation project, all graphic elements, visual identity, user research, use of methodologies, and final project were my own work.
Results
  • Experience with faculty: the material was developed with the help of one of the members of the game design course. All analysis structures and product conception respected the faculty's vision, making the adoption of the final product much more natural.
  • Service design with pedagogical focus: some service design methodologies were used, such as the Value Proposition Canvas and the Service Blueprint. However, the approach adopted followed a learning-oriented direction, seeking to understand how the information transmitted in the classroom could be presented to students more efficiently and meaningfully.
  • Applied design thinking: Throughout the work, various methodologies were applied with the aim of valuing the user's perspective. Personas, scenarios, journeys, among other tools, were used to expand the universe surrounding the user, culminating in the creation of one of the developed products: the Cellular Diagram.
  • Grounded product: the first idea was to develop an app. However, this solution did not suit the end user of the product. Therefore, the research was respected and the product followed the surveys done, which in the end turned into an interactive PDF.

( 3 ) Main challenges

Almost a game-over

Challenge
  • Weakened by difficult times: due to the Covid-19 pandemic, the course, which integrated two campuses and four different programs, was in a risky situation. Its schedule had been disrupted, resulting in the absence of the course offering for two years.
  • Opposing schedules: the delivery of this final work and the next offering of the course did not coincide, making direct application of the product impossible. Thus, the test would only be conducted after the conception of the final product.
  • Tic-tac: there were less than four months to deliver the final project report, a visual identity, all pedagogical directional materials (MDPs), and a UIkit for future adjustments.
Solution
  • Simple and clear graphic interfaces: More practical to develop and demand less visual elaboration time, adopting usual color palettes and navigation flows already consolidated by leading companies in the market.
  • Understanding the history: all research conducted in this work focused on a qualitative approach. Several interviews were conducted with students who had taken the course one or more times, as well as conversations with its organizers.
  • The most important first: initially, only some initial MDPs would be developed to be tested in the course. After this stage, adjustments and versioning of the material would be made, opening space for the continuity and evolution of the project.

( 4 ) Gathering information

Understanding the user's cosmos

Problem After conducting an extensive study, including research, interviews, official and unofficial readings, a central challenge emerged: how to transform all this theoretical foundation into practical application? What kind of product could emerge from this knowledge? How to connect theory, student experience, and the needs of the course in a coherent and useful way?
Solution Much of the UX was based on Jakob's Law. There was no time or personnel to develop all elements, so it was necessary to adopt functionalities that had already been tested and refined. Elements and guidelines from Excel, Kayak, Ifood, and other market giants were essential for the conception of assets, grids, and system behaviors.
The cellular diagram, also nicknamed as the Diagram of Integrating Elements of a Pedagogical Material Linked to the Service Design Approach (DEIAMPLADS), but let's stick with cellular diagram... it's more practical.

( 6 ) Gathering information 2: the return

A bit more information

Important findings
  • Needs to be easily accessible: nothing too fancy. The product should be simple and straightforward, without requiring the download of additional apps or frequent updates, as this could compromise the user experience. Furthermore, it was essential to ensure broad compatibility, avoiding problems that could hinder its use.
  • Lightweight file: no heavy files. It would possibly be shared among students in messaging apps.
  • Mobile-friendly: on the way to college, rereading that important part on the bus or subway.
  • Precise information: no fluff, the minimum with a lot to say. Straight to the point.

( 7 ) MDP

the user's cosmos

The Pedagogical Directional Material (MDP) is an interactive PDF developed to support the practical learning of digital game development, presenting essential concepts of the course in a clear, accessible way and adapted for mobile devices. Created with a focus on student autonomy and ease of use by the teacher, the MDP works as a compact, dynamic, and lightweight guide that can be used without the need for internet, website, or app. Its main objective is to direct learning in a structured and visual way, facilitating the understanding of the processes involved in creating a digital game, from rules and mechanics to animation and visual style.
The MDP is divided into cards that organize the content by complexity scale.

( 8 ) Finalization

Thanks for playing!

Although it's a relatively simple project, the rich part, and at the time complex to synthesize, was the research. Research is rewarding and structures the skeleton of complex projects. It assists in decision-making and was essential for the logical construction of my project. I leave here my thanks to the volunteers who accepted my interview requests and of course to the project's evaluation panel. Here is the link to the complete project: Complete project - bdm.unb.br
Learnings
  • Simplicity is important: a beautiful app isn't always a functional app. Prioritizing the essential and bringing innovative solutions is more attractive than beautiful elements thrown on the screen.
  • There's more beyond apps and web: opening your mind to the possibilities of human-computer interaction is necessary. In a world with so many constant changes, sometimes the most practical path is the most innovative one.
  • Research visualization is necessary: a multi-page research paper will hardly be read, but an infographic is always a more practical option. Information flows faster and the structuring of ideas is more dynamic.
  • UX prioritization: once you understand who needs you, it becomes much easier to help that person. Understanding the user's universe is vital for developing functional products.
